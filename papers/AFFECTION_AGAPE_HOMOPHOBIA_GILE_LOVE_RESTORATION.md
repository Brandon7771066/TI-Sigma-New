## CORE THESIS

**Before ~1900s:** Deep affection in friendships was normal. People hugged, cuddled, held hands, and showed physical warmth to close friends regardless of gender. These expressions were **phenomenologically indistinguishable from romance** but socially distinguished by clear boundaries: people knew friendship from romance.

**After ~1900s:** Homophobia and shame caused wholesale withdrawal of affection. Society demanded larger social boundaries. Result: **Plummeting of genuine love** and rising meanness/isolation.

**Brandon's Claim:** Agape (universal love) springs naturally and reliably from affection—not from cold obligation or reward-punishment systems.

## Part 1: The Historical Record

### 1.1 Pre-1900s Affection Norms

**Documented practices among close friends:**
- Extended hugging and physical holding
- Cuddling during social time
- Playful physical contact (including spanking as friendship expression)
- Hand-holding in public
- Sleeping in same bed (common and innocent)
- Extended eye contact and emotional intimacy

**Key distinction:** These were clearly friendship, not romance. Everyone understood:
- Affection ≠ Sexuality
- Physical closeness ≠ Sexual contact
- Love for friend ≠ Romantic love

**Historical evidence:**
- Letters between same-sex friends expressing intense love/affection
- Social customs allowing physical warmth across same-sex friendships
- Photographs showing men arm-in-arm, women holding hands as normal
- Literature describing "romantic friendships" that were non-sexual but deeply affectionate

### 1.2 The Homophobia Shift

**What changed:**
- Rise of homophobia (late 1800s-1900s)
- Pathologization of same-sex touch
- Association of all same-sex affection with sexuality
- Social shame and anxiety about physical expression
- Erosion of trust in same-sex friendships

**The mechanism:**
The fear that affection = homosexuality caused society to:
1. **Criminalize the affection** (not the sexuality, the warmth itself)
2. **Pathologize touching** (physical closeness became suspicious)
3. **Demand rigid boundaries** (distance became safety)
4. **Reserve affection for romance only** (one designated person per adult)

### 1.3 The Boundaries Paradox

| Era | Affection Flow | Boundaries | Social Trust |
|-----|----------------|-----------|--------------|
| Pre-1900s | Natural & abundant | Thin but clear | High |
| Post-1900s | Withdrawn & scarce | Thick & rigid | Low |

**Key insight:** Thin boundaries ≠ confused boundaries.

Pre-1900s society had:
- ✅ Abundant physical affection
- ✅ **Clear distinction** between friendship and romance
- ✅ Higher social trust
- ✅ Genuine expressions of care

Modern society has:
- ❌ Scarce, guarded affection
- ❌ Anxious about intimacy (what if it's misread?)
- ❌ Lower social trust
- ❌ Affection replaced by obligation
