## D.1 Purpose and Methodology

This section evaluates the Existence Intensity (Ξ) framework against three historically distinct market crises:

- **1929** — structural constraint accumulation without stabilizing institutions

**Each crisis corresponds to a Ξ-phase transition that occurred before the visible price collapse.**

We compare Ξ-phase diagnostics to conventional metrics (volatility, returns, macro indicators) and show systematic lead–lag differences.

## D.3 Case Study I — The 1929 Crash

### Slow Constraint Accumulation, No Reset Mechanism

**Ξ-Phase Reconstruction:**

### Expansion (1924–1927)
- Low constraint, high permissibility
- Credit expansion opens futures faster than they close
- PD broad and centered

### Compression (1928–mid 1929)
- Rising leverage constrains downside options
- PD narrows sharply
- Volatility remains low → **false sense of safety**

### Fracture (Oct 1929)
- Sudden collapse of admissible futures
- Liquidity disappears
- Extreme negative Ξ dominates cumulative impact

### Reset (1932 onward)
- Constraint released only through massive destruction of capital
- PD broadens at a much lower baseline

**Key Insight:**

Standard models failed because:
- Volatility remained suppressed until fracture
- No mechanism tracked constraint buildup

Ξ succeeds because:
- Compression is visible as PD narrowing
- Fracture occurs when constraint exceeds relaxation capacity

### Memory-Locked Fracture in an Interconnected System

- True constraint hidden in balance-sheet coupling

- PD narrows across asset classes
- κ for negative housing signals increases

- Small shocks propagate globally

- PD broadens unevenly (asset inflation without structural repair)

**Key Insight:**

2008 illustrates **κ-dominant crises**:
- Negative experiences persist across institutions
- Constraint remains elevated even after price recovery

This explains why:
- Volatility normalized faster than risk
- "Recovery" felt fragile for years

- Fracture need not arise endogenously
- Reset can be imposed, not earned

Ξ correctly distinguishes:
- Natural resets (1929, 2008) from

**This distinction matters for long-term risk.**
