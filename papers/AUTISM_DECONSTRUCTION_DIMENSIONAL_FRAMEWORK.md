## PART 2: THE THREE-HIT METABOLIC MODEL AS CAUSAL STRUCTURE

### 2.1 Why the Three-Hit Model Changes Everything

**Standard view:** Autism = genetic disorder (or environmental, or both—unclear)

**UCSD Naviaux finding:** Autism results from three aligned conditions:
1. **Genetic predisposition** (sensitive mitochondria/signaling)
2. **Environmental trigger** (infection, immune stress, pollution)
3. **Prolonged CDR activation** (cell danger response stuck in "on" position)

**Critical insight:** The CDR (cell danger response) is the mechanism linking all three.

### 2.2 The Cell Danger Response (CDR)

**Normal CDR:**
- Turns on in response to threat/injury
- Activates cellular defense
- Turns off once threat passes
- Normal, protective

**Chronic CDR:**
- Stays activated due to:
  - Repeated/ongoing stressors
  - Inherited hypersensitivity to threat signals
  - Dysregulated purinergic signaling (abnormal ATP)
- Diverts resources from development to defense
- Disrupts brain circuit formation during critical periods
- Results in autism features

### 2.3 The Purinergic Signaling Problem

**Key mechanism:** Extracellular ATP (eATP) carries stress signals between cells

**In chronic CDR:**
- eATP stays elevated
- Cells interpret this as ongoing threat
- Purinergic receptors remain activated
- Cellular communication breaks down
- Neural circuit formation impaired

**Brandon's insight:** This is **QUANTIFIABLE and REVERSIBLE** in principle.

### 2.4 Why This Is Revolutionary

**Traditional neurology:** Brain circuit problems = structural, permanent

**Three-hit model:** Brain circuit problems = metabolic dysfunction, potentially reversible

**Implication:** If you calm the CDR early enough, you can restore normal brain development—even partially in older cases.

## PART 3: REPLACING AUTISM WITH DIMENSIONAL SUBTYPES

### 3.1 Six Core Dimensions (Replacing Single "Autism" Label)

#### **A. Sensory Processing Profile**

**Independent axes:**
- **Hyper-reactive:** Sensory input feels overwhelming (sounds too loud, lights too bright)
- **Hypo-reactive:** Sensory input feels dampened (seeking intense stimuli)
- **Context-sensitive:** Varies by situation/state
- **Mixed/unstable:** Fluctuates unpredictably

**What this predicts:**
- Environmental accommodation needs
- Burnout vulnerability
- Regulation capacity
- Optimal therapy setting (quiet office vs. engaging environment)

**NOT predicted by "autism diagnosis"**

---

#### **B. Social Motivation Profile** (Not "Social Ability")

**Crucial distinction:** Desire for connection ≠ skill at execution

**Profiles:**
- **Low desire / low distress:** "I'm fine alone"
- **High desire / high distress:** "I want friends but don't know how; feels painful"
- **Context-dependent:** "With specific people I'm comfortable, otherwise withdrawn"
- **Avoidant due to overload:** "I want connection but sensory/social overload forces withdrawal"

**This explains more variance than "autism severity."**

---

#### **C. Cognitive Style & Abstraction Profile**

**Independent of IQ:**
- **Bottom-up vs. top-down:** Do you detail-first or big-picture-first?
- **Rule-based vs. intuition-based:** Do you apply rules mechanically or sense context?
- **Flexible vs. rigid:** Can you shift mental set easily?
- **Pattern recognition style:** What kinds of patterns catch your eye?

**What this predicts:**
- Optimal learning modality
- Career/occupational fit
- Which therapy approaches work
- How to present information

---

#### **D. Emotional Interoception & Meaning Access**

**Often mislabeled as "alexithymia" (lack of emotion words):**

**Actual profiles:**
- **Clear access:** "I know exactly what I'm feeling and can name it"
- **Delayed access:** "I feel something, takes time to process and understand"
- **Cognitive-only access:** "I understand logically what I should feel, but sensation is absent"
- **Overwhelming access:** "I feel everything intensely and can't sort signals"

**What this predicts:**
- Therapy responsiveness (some therapies require emotional awareness)
- Risk of depression/anxiety masquerading as autism
- Identity coherence ("do I know myself?")
- Medication response

---

#### **E. Regulation Bandwidth**

**How much stress a nervous system can absorb before collapse:**

**Profiles:**
- **High baseline regulation:** "I stay calm through chaos"
- **Narrow bandwidth:** "I dysregulate quickly under any pressure"
- **Fluctuating capacity:** "Sometimes resilient, sometimes fragile; depends on sleep/stress"
- **Context-sensitive stability:** "Different environments have different thresholds"

**What this predicts:**
- Real-world functioning (more important than IQ or "severity")
- Relapse risk
- Environmental accommodation needs
- Preventive intervention strategy

**NOT predicted by diagnosis; often mistaken for "functioning level."**

---

#### **F. Developmental History & Adaptation Strategy**

**Two people with identical traits diverge radically based on:**
- **Early scaffolding:** Did caregivers understand and support your needs?
- **Trauma exposure:** Abuse, neglect, medical trauma?
- **Masking vs. accommodation:** Did you hide yourself or did environment adapt?
- **Reinforcement patterns:** Were your strengths celebrated or punished?

**What this predicts:**
- Current identity stability
- Shame/internalized stigma level
- Capacity for change
- Risk of secondary anxiety/depression
- Treatment timeline

## PART 4: INTEGRATING THE THREE-HIT MODEL

### 4.1 Mapping Dimensional Profiles to Three-Hit Causation

**Key insight:** Different sensory/social/cognitive profiles may reflect different hit patterns.

#### **Pattern 1: High Genetic Predisposition + Severe Trigger + Early CDR Activation**
- **Result:** Early-onset, severe features
- **Dimensional profile:** Often narrow regulation bandwidth, multiple sensory hypersensitivities
- **Example:** Regression-trajectory autism (appears fine at birth, loses skills 12-18 months post-trigger)
- **Three-hit mapping:** Strong genetic ↔ strong trigger = strong CDR response

#### **Pattern 2: Moderate Genetic + Cumulative Triggers + Delayed CDR Activation**
- **Result:** Later-onset, variable features, masking compensations
- **Dimensional profile:** Often high social desire with distress, good cognitive function
- **Example:** "Camouflaging" autistic (seems fine until burnout)
- **Three-hit mapping:** Moderate genetic ↔ repeated stressors = slow CDR buildup

#### **Pattern 3: High Genetic + Minimal Trigger + Idiosyncratic CDR**
- **Result:** Lifelong traits, not "disorder," variable distress
- **Dimensional profile:** Often low social desire (non-distressed), distinctive cognitive strength
- **Example:** Brilliant programmer who "doesn't want socializing"—that's not suffering
- **Three-hit mapping:** Strong genetic alone insufficient; may not have had the trigger

### 4.2 Why This Changes Prevention

**40-50% of autism cases preventable because:**
1. **Hits 2 & 3 are modifiable** (triggers and CDR activation)
2. **Early detection possible** (metabolomic profiling pre-symptom)
3. **CDR can be calmed** (anti-purinergic therapy + metabolic support)
