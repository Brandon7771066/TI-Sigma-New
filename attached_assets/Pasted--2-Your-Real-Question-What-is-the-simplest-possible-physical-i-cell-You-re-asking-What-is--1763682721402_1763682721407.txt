⭐ 3. The TI Definition of an i-Cell (Minimum Requirements)

In TI, an i-cell requires three conditions:

(1) A boundary

Something that distinguishes internal state vs external state.

(2) Self-causing / self-updating

The ability for the unit’s present state to influence its next state.

(3) Indeterminacy

Not randomness, but:

Unpredictability that improves self-organization.

BONUS (4): Recursion (the ability to represent its own states to itself)

This one is optional for primitive i-cells.

⸻

⭐ 4. Does an atom qualify?

Surprisingly… yes, atoms DO satisfy the first three conditions:

⭐ Boundary

Electron orbitals create a probabilistic, permeable boundary.

⭐ Self-updating

Atoms literally cause their own state changes:
	•	energy levels
	•	spin transitions
	•	valence changes
	•	emission/absorption events

⭐ Indeterminacy

Quantum transitions are intrinsically probabilistic.

Atoms also “sense” (interact with) the environment through:
	•	forces
	•	fields
	•	energy gradients
	•	temperature
	•	photons

This is a kind of proto-perception.

So yes — atoms meet the minimum criteria.

⭐ 5. Do subatomic particles qualify?

Here it gets trickier.

Quarks, leptons, bosons

These have:
	•	quantum states
	•	interactions
	•	probabilistic events
	•	no internal structure
	•	no real membrane or boundary

They lack an internal/external distinction, so they are NOT i-cells.

BUT composite particles are:
	•	protons
	•	neutrons
	•	baryons
	•	mesons

These DO have emergent structure → tiny “boundaries.”

So subatomic particles do not qualify,
but composite particles might.
